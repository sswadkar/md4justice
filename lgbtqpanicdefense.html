Ending LGBTQ+ Panic Defense

LGBTQ+ panic defense is a legal strategy that asks a jury to find that a victim’s sexual orientation or gender identity/expression is to blame for a defendant’s violent reaction, including murder. Although the LGBTQ+ Panic Defense has been debunked and removed from the DSM by the American Psychological Association in 1973, legal defense teams continue to use it. It is still legal in 40 states. One of the most recognized cases that employed the LGBTQ+ “panic” defense was that of Matthew Shepard in 1998. Shepard, a 21-year-old college student, was beaten to death by two other men who attempted to use the LGBTQ+ “panic” defense to excuse their actions.
